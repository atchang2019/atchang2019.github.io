The Powerhouse of the Cell

The mitochondria is the powerhouse of the cell. The mitochondria is where cellular respiration, the biochemical pathway which creates ATP from glucose, occurs. Cellular respiration involves multiple different processes including glycolysis, the intermediate step, the Kreb's cycle, and the electron transport chain. In glycolysis, which occurs in the cytoplasm, an initial 2 ATP is invested to break down 1 glucose molecule into 2 pyruvate. A glucose molecule is a 6-carbon molecule, so it splits into two 3-carbon chains called pyruvate.


Then, in the intermediate step, these pyruvate molecules are oxidized and turned into acetyl-CoA. In the intermediate step, each pyruvate molecule loses a carbon atom to an oxygen molecule to make a carbon dioxide molecule and loses an electron to a NAD+, which becomes a NADH molecule (an electron carrier). After the intermediate step is the Kreb's cycle, where the acetyl-CoA goes in as a reactant. The products of the Kreb's cycle are 8 NADH, 2 FADH2, 2 ATP, and 6 CO2 (some of the FADH and CO2 that are included in these figures come from the intermediate step). The next step occurs in the electron transport chain, where the electron carriers release hydrogen ions, which are pumped out into the intermembrane space by hydrogen pump proteins imbedded in the cristae. This creates a natural concentration gradient for the hydrogen ions to travel down. This pumping of hydrogens takes very little energy, and the result of these flowing down their concentration gradient yields a high amount of energy. When these hydrogens flow down the concentration gradient, they travel through the ATP synthase, releasing high amounts of energy and powering the turning of this motor protein. The ATP synthase, using this released energy, essentially smushes the ADP and phosphate group together, forming ATP. This ATP then can be used by the cell in other biochemical processes or to simply store energy.

The last little step in this entire process of cell respiration is that oxygen molecules come to pick up the hydrogen ions that have already traveled through the ATP synthase, in order to maintain the high concentration gradient to continue powering the synthesis of ATP. When the electrons attach to the O2 molecules, they break up into two oxygen atoms, allowing two hydrogens to attach to each oxygen atom, creating H2O. GO MITOCHONDRIA!
